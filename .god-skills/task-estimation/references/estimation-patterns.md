# Estimation Patterns

## Core idea

A useful estimate is a planning signal tied to a decision, not a performance
promise disguised as a number.

## Common estimation modes

- Story points: relative sizing for near-term team planning
- T-shirt sizing: quick backlog or roadmap sorting
- Planning poker prep: candidate range plus disagreement areas before the team
  votes
- Rough forecast: range plus confidence for planning conversations
- Needs split first: the work is too broad to size honestly as one item

## Repeated failure modes

1. Precision theater: a confident number without clear scope
2. Hidden work: testing, rollout, approvals, or documentation omitted from the
   estimate
3. No reference anchor: the team sizes in isolation instead of comparing to
   known work
4. Scope and estimate mixed together: the item still needs decomposition
5. Estimate-as-deadline: stakeholders treat a planning signal as a commitment

## Reference-story calibration

Anchor estimates against one or two known pieces of work:

- similar technical surface
- similar coordination cost
- similar uncertainty level
- similar review or QA burden

If no usable reference exists, lower confidence and explain why.

## Split signals

Recommend a split when:

- one item spans multiple systems or owner roles
- discovery and delivery are bundled together
- rollout, migration, or compliance work is hidden inside the estimate
- the team cannot explain why the item is bigger than nearby reference stories

## Decision reminders

### Sprint selection

Prefer story points or planning-poker prep. Call out blockers that would make
the item not ready even if the size feels reasonable.

### Roadmap sorting

Prefer t-shirt sizing or rough forecast ranges over fake date precision.

### Spike or research work

Estimate the exploration boundary, not the full downstream implementation.
